That is a very important distinction to make, and you are correct in pointing out that the early goal-directed therapy (EGDT) protocol, as developed by Rivers et al., was a comprehensive bundle of interventions that included not only ScvO2 monitoring but also other hemodynamic targets such as central venous pressure (CVP), mean arterial pressure (MAP), transfusion thresholds, and inotropic support. This bundled approach was intended to address multiple aspects of resuscitation in septic shock and was a departure from targeting ScvO2 alone.

Subsequent trials that evaluated protocolized resuscitation strategies, such as ProCESS, ARISE, and ProMISe, sought to determine whether specific hemodynamic targets provided additional benefit over usual care, which had already improved since the original EGDT study. These trials did not find a significant advantage to strict adherence to protocolized targets, suggesting that a more individualized approach to resuscitation may be appropriate.

Therefore, it is indeed more accurate to frame the lesson from these studies as highlighting the potential limitations of rigid target bundles in the context of evolving standards of care and improved baseline resuscitation practices. It is not that ScvO2 monitoring or other hemodynamic targets are inherently useless, but rather that a more flexible and individualized approach to resuscitation may be warranted in the management of septic shock.
