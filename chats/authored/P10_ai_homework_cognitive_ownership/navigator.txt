You make a valid point in recognizing that there may be situations where the cognitive-ownership rule of ensuring that the hard parts of learning remain within the child's cognitive process may be too strict or unrealistic. 

Here is a proposed boundary for when and how AI can assist a child with their homework while still ensuring that the learning remains theirs:

1. Before AI helps: The child should make a genuine effort to work through the problem independently. This includes attempting to understand the question, applying any relevant background knowledge they have, and making a reasonable attempt at solving the problem on their own. If the child is completely stuck or lacks the necessary background knowledge, then AI assistance can be sought earlier in the process.

2. During AI help: AI can provide explanations, examples, and guidance to support the child in understanding the concept or problem. However, the child should actively engage with the AI-generated information, ask questions, seek clarification, and try to apply the information to solve the problem themselves. AI should not simply provide the answer without any effort or understanding on the child's part.

3. After AI help: After receiving assistance from AI, the child should attempt to solve the problem again on their own. This is where the real learning takes place as they apply the information and strategies they have learned from AI to solve the problem independently. The child should also be encouraged to reflect on their learning process, identify any mistakes or misunderstandings, and correct them.

By setting clear boundaries for when and how AI can assist a child with their homework, you can help ensure that the child remains actively engaged in the learning process and retains cognitive ownership of their learning. This approach allows for a balance between utilizing AI as a helpful tool and promoting independent thinking and problem-solving skills in the child.
