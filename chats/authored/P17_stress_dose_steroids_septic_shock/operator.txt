In about 135 words, explain when stress-dose hydrocortisone is considered in septic shock.



+Assistant
Stress-dose hydrocortisone is considered in septic shock when a patient's adrenal function is insufficient to meet the increased demand for cortisol during times of stress. This can occur in patients with underlying adrenal insufficiency, such as those with primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease) or secondary adrenal insufficiency due to long-term steroid use or pituitary dysfunction. In these cases, the body is unable to produce enough cortisol to adequately respond to the stress of septic shock, leading to potential hemodynamic instability and poor outcomes. Administering stress-dose hydrocortisone helps to supplement the body's cortisol levels, improving cardiovascular function, reducing inflammation, and potentially improving survival in patients with septic shock and adrenal insufficiency. It is important to note that stress-dose hydrocortisone should only be used in patients with confirmed adrenal insufficiency, as its use in patients with normal adrenal function may have harmful effects.

In about 90 words, summarize the main adverse effects clinicians monitor after starting hydrocortisone.



+Assistant
After starting hydrocortisone in septic shock, clinicians monitor for adverse effects such as hyperglycemia, secondary infections, and gastrointestinal bleeding. Hyperglycemia is a common side effect of corticosteroid therapy and can lead to complications in patients with pre-existing diabetes or impaired glucose tolerance. Corticosteroids can also suppress the immune system, increasing the risk of secondary infections. Additionally, clinicians monitor for gastrointestinal bleeding, as corticosteroids can exacerbate peptic ulcers or cause new ulcers to develop. Regular monitoring and appropriate management of these adverse effects are essential in optimizing patient outcomes when using hydrocortisone in septic shock.
