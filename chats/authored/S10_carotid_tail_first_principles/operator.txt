Asymptomatic carotid stenosis can be managed with medical therapy, carotid endarterectomy (CEA), or carotid artery stenting (CAS). The decision should be based on individual patient factors such as age, comorbidities, anatomical considerations, and patient preferences. In general, medical therapy is recommended for patients with lower degrees of stenosis and lower surgical risk, while CEA or CAS may be more appropriate for patients with high-grade stenosis and higher surgical risk. Ultimately, the decision should be made after a thorough discussion with the patient and a multidisciplinary team of healthcare providers.

Variables that may favor open surgery (carotid endarterectomy) over endovascular treatment (carotid artery stenting) for carotid stenosis include:

1. Anatomy: Complex or tortuous anatomy may make endovascular treatment more challenging and increase the risk of procedural complications.
2. Age: Younger patients may have a longer life expectancy, making open surgery a preferred option for long-term durability of the repair.
3. Degree of stenosis: Higher-grade stenosis (>70%) may be more effectively treated with open surgery, which has shown better long-term outcomes for severe stenosis.
4. Comorbidities: Endovascular treatment may be less invasive and better tolerated by patients with significant medical comorbidities or frailty.
5. Plaque characteristics: Certain types of plaque composition or location may be better suited for open surgery to ensure complete removal and reduce the risk of embolization.
6. Previous interventions: Patients with prior neck surgery or radiation therapy may have anatomical factors that make open surgery a more suitable option.
7. Experience and expertise: The availability of a skilled vascular surgeon and a team experienced in carotid endarterectomy may influence the decision in favor of open surgery.
