You make an important point about the potential failure modes of using sports as a youth drug-prevention infrastructure. If sports become elite-only, expensive, injury-prone, or exclusive to only the most athletic individuals, it can lead to exclusion and marginalization of less-athletic or disadvantaged youth. This can undermine the goal of creating a broad and inclusive belonging infrastructure that serves as a protective factor against risky behaviors like drug use.

To ensure that sports remain accessible and protective for all young people, policymakers and stakeholders can consider the following design principles:

1. **Equity and Inclusivity:** Ensure that sports programs are accessible to all youth, regardless of their socio-economic background, athletic ability, or physical condition. This may involve providing financial assistance, scholarships, and opportunities for those who may face barriers to participation.

2. **Diversity and Representation:** Promote diversity and inclusion in sports by offering a variety of activities and programs that cater to different interests and abilities. Celebrate and value participation and effort, rather than just focusing on competitive success.

3. **Safety and Well-being:** Prioritize the safety and well-being of young athletes by providing proper training, supervision, and equipment to reduce the risk of injuries. Create a supportive and positive environment that emphasizes physical and emotional health.

4. **Skill-building and Personal Growth:** Emphasize the development of life skills, character traits, and personal growth through sports participation. Focus on teamwork, leadership, resilience, and goal-setting to empower young people beyond just athletic performance.

5. **Community Engagement:** Foster a sense of community and belonging through sports by encouraging collaboration with schools, local organizations, and community partners. Create opportunities for mentorship, volunteering, and social connections that extend beyond the sports field.

By incorporating these design principles into sports programs and policies, countries can ensure that sports serve as a broad and inclusive protective factor for youth, rather than a selective or exclusionary system. This approach can help create a supportive and empowering environment that promotes positive behaviors and choices among all young people, especially those who may be most vulnerable to the risks of drug use.
