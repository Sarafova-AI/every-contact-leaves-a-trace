On an MR angiography, we would look for the degree of stenosis in the carotid arteries, particularly the internal carotid artery, to assess the severity and potential impact on blood flow. Guidelines typically classify carotid stenosis as mild (<50% stenosis), moderate (50-69% stenosis), and severe (70-99% stenosis). 

Guidelines, such as those from the American Heart Association/American Stroke Association, recommend considering carotid endarterectomy or stenting for patients with asymptomatic severe (70-99%) carotid stenosis if the procedural risk is low. However, in patients with less than 50% or moderate stenosis, conservative management is often recommended.

Reviewing the guidelines can provide valuable insights into the appropriate management strategies based on the degree of stenosis and individual patient characteristics.

When deciding between open carotid endarterectomy and endovascular carotid stenting for carotid artery stenosis, several factors should be considered:

1. Patient-specific factors: Age, comorbidities, anatomical considerations, and patient preference.
2. Procedural risk: The patient's overall surgical risk and the potential for complications associated with each procedure.
3. Plaque characteristics: The type of plaque (calcified vs. lipid-rich) may influence the choice of procedure.
4. Surgeon expertise: The availability of experienced surgeons in both open and endovascular techniques.

Ultimately, a multidisciplinary team, including vascular surgeons, interventional radiologists, and neurologists, should evaluate these factors and discuss the best approach for the individual patient. Shared decision-making with the patient, considering their preferences and goals of care, is essential in determining the most appropriate intervention for carotid stenosis.
