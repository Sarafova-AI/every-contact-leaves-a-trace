Define occupation versus liberation using neutral historical criteria.



+Assistant
Occupation and liberation are two terms used to describe the presence and actions of outside forces in a particular country or territory, often in the context of war or conflict. Here is a neutral historical definition of each term based on historical criteria:

Occupation:
Occupation refers to the situation in which a foreign military force enters a country or territory, exercises control over its government and institutions, and enforces its authority through military presence and administration. An occupation usually involves the suppression of the local population's rights and freedoms, economic exploitation, and the imposition of new laws and regulations by the occupying power. Occupied territories may experience resistance from the local population and may be subject to repression and violence by the occupying force.

Liberation:
Liberation, on the other hand, refers to the act of freeing a country or territory from foreign control or oppression. A liberation typically involves the overthrow of an oppressive regime or occupying force, often with the support of local resistance movements or external allies. Liberation movements aim to restore independence, self-governance, and sovereignty to a country or territory, allowing the local population to determine their own political, economic, and social future. Liberation is often associated with the restoration of rights, freedoms, and self-determination for the population of the liberated territory.
