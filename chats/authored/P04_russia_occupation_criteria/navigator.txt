Occupation and liberation are distinct concepts that describe different types of interventions or actions by external forces in a country or territory. Here are the definitions and criteria for each:

Occupation:
- Occupation refers to the presence and control of foreign military forces in a country or territory without the consent of the sovereign government or local population.
- Occupation often involves the use of force or coercion to establish control over the occupied territory.
- Occupying forces may impose their own political, economic, and social systems on the occupied territory and may suppress local institutions and authorities.

Liberation:
- Liberation refers to the freeing of a country or territory from oppressive or unwanted control, often by external forces.
- Liberation is typically seen as a process that restores or establishes national sovereignty and independence for the liberated country or territory.
- Liberating forces are often viewed as allies or supporters of the local population in their struggle for self-determination and freedom from oppression.

Criteria for Occupation:
- Presence of foreign military forces in the territory without the consent of the local government or population.
- Imposition of control, laws, and policies by the occupying forces.
- Suppression of local institutions and authorities.

Criteria for Liberation:
- Removal of oppressive or unwanted control over the country or territory.
- Restoration or establishment of national sovereignty and independence.
- Support for self-determination and freedom for the local population.

It is important to differentiate between occupation and liberation in analyzing historical events and their impacts on countries and societies. The context, motives, and consequences of external interventions can help determine whether a situation is characterized by occupation or liberation.
