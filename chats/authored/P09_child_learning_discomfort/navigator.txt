To effectively guide a child through learning discomfort and keep them in the optimal learning zone, the adult needs to regulate several key factors:

1. **Task Difficulty**: The adult should adjust the difficulty of the task to match the child's current skill level. The goal is to provide a challenge that is just beyond the child's comfort zone, pushing them to grow without overwhelming them.

2. **Time Under Strain**: The adult should be mindful of the amount of time the child spends struggling with the task. It is important to allow for productive struggle, but also to intervene if the child is becoming too frustrated or stressed.

3. **Emotional Safety**: Creating a safe and supportive environment is crucial for learning. The adult should validate the child's feelings of frustration and offer encouragement and reassurance that it's okay to struggle and make mistakes.

4. **Help Timing**: Knowing when to provide help or guidance is key. The adult should observe the child's progress and provide assistance at the right moment to facilitate learning without taking over the task completely.

5. **Child's Interpretation of Struggle**: The adult can help the child reframe their interpretation of struggle as a natural part of the learning process. Encouraging a growth mindset, where challenges are seen as opportunities for growth, can help the child embrace learning discomfort.

To keep the child inside the learning zone without rescuing too early or pushing into shame, the adult should:

1. **Encourage Effort**: Praise the child's effort and persistence, rather than just focusing on the outcome. This reinforces the idea that hard work and perseverance are valuable qualities.

2. **Provide Guidance**: Offer hints, suggestions, or alternative strategies to help the child approach the problem from a different angle. This can help them overcome obstacles without feeling overwhelmed.

3. **Normalize Struggle**: Normalize the idea that learning can be challenging and that it's okay to feel frustrated or stuck at times. Emphasize that mistakes are a natural part of the learning process.

4. **Celebrate Progress**: Acknowledge and celebrate small victories and incremental progress. This can boost the child's confidence and motivation to continue working through difficult tasks.

By regulating these factors and maintaining a balance between challenge and support, the adult can help the child navigate learning discomfort in a way that promotes growth, resilience, and a positive attitude towards learning.
